## Summary

Widely used global reference chapter that lays out the **dispensing cycle/
process** step by step and is especially relevant to lower-resource settings.
It treats dispensing as a discrete clinical-operational process (not merely
handing over a box) and defines good dispensing practice as getting "the right
medicine, to the right patient, in the right dose and quantity, with clear

## Key claims

- Defines the **dispensing process** as a sequence: (1) **receive and validate**
  the prescription; (2) **understand and interpret** the prescription; (3)
  **prepare and label** items for issue; (4) make a **final check**; (5)
  **record** the action taken; (6) **issue medicine to the patient with clear
  instructions and advice** (counselling).
- The goal of good dispensing is the **right medicine, to the right patient, in
  the right dose and quantity, with clear instructions, and in a package that
  preserves the potency of the medicine**.
- Dispensing errors arise from look-alike/sound-alike names, poor handwriting,
  ambiguous abbreviations, calculation errors, distraction, and high workload.
- A **suitable dispensing environment** (adequate space, lighting, clean
  surfaces, low distraction, organised stock) reduces error.
- Counselling and labelling are core to dispensing, not optional add-ons; the
  patient must leave knowing what the medicine is, how and when to take it, and
  what to expect.

## Verbatim excerpts

> "Good dispensing ensures that an effective form of the correct medicine is
