# Summary

Internal **primary qualitative research** for Dis-Chem's **Store of the Future
(SOTF)** programme. Twelve frequent Dis-Chem pharmacy customers were interviewed
(remote, ~1 hour each, 26–28 January 2026) to explore how they perceive and
respond to **proposed future-state dispensary journeys**, using scenario-based
exploration across four scenarios: (S1) going to the pharmacy/clinic, (S2)
pre-ordering & collecting, (S3) delivery & ongoing managed medication, (S4)
communication, support & reassurance. The study tested **sentiment, confidence
and concerns** — not final designs or operational execution.

**Reliability — B (qualitative, small-n; justify):** company primary research,
methodologically sound (screener, protocol, consent/NDA, ~12 interview-hours,
note-taking + synthesis), and directly on-topic for the future-state vision. But
n=12 and qualitative, so directional, not statistically representative: the
"X/12" splits below are **interview tallies, not population estimates** and must
be framed as such. Treated as a strong **signal** of customer preference, above
D-tier review/complaint sentiment, below A-tier quantified study.

The headline thesis: customers value **predictability, reassurance and recovery
over speed or novelty**; digital is welcomed **only when it reinforces
confidence and keeps a visible human fallback**, not when it transfers
responsibility or removes control.

# Key claims (each a checkable fact about what the study found)

## Method & participants
- **Method:** remote in-depth interviews, ~1 hour each, scenario-based;
  **n = 12** frequent Dis-Chem pharmacy customers. Fieldwork **26–28 Jan 2026**;
  report dated **2026-02-06**. Researcher: **Camilla Cicatello**.
- **Screener:** visited a Dis-Chem pharmacy **4+ times in the past 6 months**;
  mix of **chronic and acute** needs; **5 of 12** had recent experience of
  Dis-Chem's **pre-packed medication collection** service.
- **Spread:** province GP 5 / WC 3 / KZN 3 / EC 1; gender 7F (58%) / 5M (42%);
  age 25–34 = 3, 35–44 = 5, 45–55 = 2, 55+ = 2; race Black 8 (67%), White 2
  (17%), Indian 1 (8%), Coloured 1 (8%); monthly household net income <R10k = 2,
  R10k–R19,999 = 3, R20k–R39,999 = 4, R40k+ = 2, prefer-not-to-say = 1.

## Baseline behaviour & mental models
- Participants manage pharmacy errands as **everyday responsibility, often on
  behalf of others** in the household; visits are **embedded in busy routines**,
  not standalone healthcare moments.
- Dis-Chem is relied on as a **familiar, dependable default**, not re-evaluated
  each visit. Waiting and busy stores are **expected and tolerated**.
- Core mental model: **"Waiting is fine — being unsure isn't."** Uncertainty
  creates more friction than waiting. Strong aversion to **not knowing** whether
  meds are ready, how long it will take, or what to do next.
- Not anti-digital, but **confidence is conditional**: **"Digital should help me
  feel more certain, not more exposed."** Digital is trusted when it confirms
  readiness, clarifies timing and reduces follow-up; questioned when it removes
  visible human fallback, shifts responsibility onto the customer, or introduces
  ambiguity.

## Scenario 1 — going to the pharmacy or clinic
- **Booking ahead vs walk-in:** 8/12 preferred booking ahead most of the time;
  3/12 preferred walking in; 1/12 "it depends". Booking valued for reducing
  uncertainty about waiting and planning around work/family/errands. Confidence
  dropped when booking felt **rigid** or the **consequence of missing a slot**
  was unclear.
- **Ticket vs physical queue:** 11/12 preferred a **numbered ticket** — the
  strongest consensus in S1. Ticketing read as **calmer, fairer, less mental
  effort**; externalises queue management; lets them move/browse. Confidence
  dropped when **place in queue felt unsure** or progress was invisible.
- **Knowing when it's your turn:** 7/12 preferred **phone-based notifications**
  (SMS/app/message); 4/12 a **combination** (phone + screen/announcement); 1/12
  in-store screens/announcements only. Phone alerts let customers **step away
  without losing control**. Confidence dropped when alerts were screen/audio-only
  or had **no backup** if missed.
- **Missing your turn (recovery):** 9/12 explicitly said missing a turn **should
  not mean starting over**; 3/12 expressed fairness concern without a clear rule.
  Customers expect a **grace period, obvious next step, and staff discretion**;
  missing a turn is "a normal, human mistake," not to be penalised.

## Scenario 2 — pre-ordering & collecting medication
- **When to collect:** 7/12 preferred **booking a collection time**; 4/12 arrive
  when it suits them; 1/12 no strong preference. Booking valued for confirming
  **readiness** and avoiding repeat trips — but not if it locks them into rigid
  timing.
- **Collection timing:** **same-day readiness** most commonly preferred; **narrow
  1-hour windows** useful only when plans are predictable; **broader windows**
  preferred when flexibility needed. Customers prefer **flexible readiness
  signals over tightly controlled windows**.
- **Arriving to collect:** 8/12 preferred **digital check-in (QR/kiosk)** —
  signals "I'm here," reduces uncertainty about being seen, avoids awkward
  repeated explanations and standing at the counter; 4/12 preferred going
  **straight to the counter** when staff visibly available / store quiet / they
  want immediate human confirmation. **Visible, immediate human acknowledgment
  can achieve the same confidence as check-in.**

## Scenario 3 — delivery & ongoing medication
- **Delivery window:** 6/12 preferred a **broader window** (e.g. "this
  afternoon"); 4/12 a **2-hour** window; 2/12 a **1-hour** window. Broader
  windows felt more achievable and fit work-from-home/flexible routines; narrow
  windows only when someone can reliably be present or meds are urgent. Customers
  **trust delivery more when timing feels realistic rather than precise.**
- **Managed/ongoing delivery:** 8/12 **positive or open** to ongoing managed
  delivery; 4/12 **hesitant/conditional**. Openness **increases for chronic /
  repeat** medication. Appeal: less monthly admin, no need to remember refills,
  reliability for chronic. But customers want **control points** — confirmation
  before each delivery, ability to **pause/adjust**, reassurance errors can be
  corrected, and visible human support. Confidence drops when the service feels
  **automatic without checks**.

## Scenario 4 — communication, support & reassurance
- **Medication instructions:** 9/12 positive toward **digital instructions
  alongside printed labels**; 3/12 preferred **printed only**. Most wanted
  **both**. Digital valued as a re-checkable reminder (side effects, dosage,
  follow-up) accessible after leaving; print valued for immediate reference,
  reassurance and **safety/backup**. Digital should **reinforce, not replace**
  physical information; confidence drops if digital is the **only** source
  (worries about access/signal/where to find it).
- **Packaging:** 7/12 saw value in **pre-packed/organised doses**, especially for
  complex or chronic medication; 5/12 preferred **standard packaging** for
  simple/short-term meds. Strongly **context-dependent** — packaging should match
  the complexity of the medication, not be applied by default.
- **Support channel:** 8/12 preferred **remote support** (call/chat/message) for
  most questions — fits daily life, avoids return trips, handles questions that
  arise later; 4/12 preferred **in-person** for complex/sensitive issues.
  Confidence highest when customers can **choose the channel** and **escalate
  easily**; drops when it's unclear who to contact or support feels
  automated/slow.

## Synthesised "What this means for SOTF" (4 principles)
1. **Predictability matters more than speed** — customers evaluate journeys by
   how clearly they understand what's happening, not how fast things move. Reduce
   guesswork over optimising throughput.
2. **Ticketing must feel forgiving, not rigid** — design "forgiveness by
   default," visible recovery paths, and **consistency across stores**.
3. **Notifications enable trust but only with backup** — combine direct (phone)
   alerts with in-store visibility; make missed-alert consequences explicit;
   always signal the next step. Notifications should **reduce** customer
   responsibility, not transfer it.
4. **Digital should support reassurance, not replace people** — digital should
   reinforce clarity, coexist with visible human support, and let customers pick
   the path that feels safest in the moment.
