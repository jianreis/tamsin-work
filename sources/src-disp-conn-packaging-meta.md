## Summary

The key meta-analytic evidence that adherence packaging works: pooling 52
reports across ~22,858 subjects, packaging interventions (blister packs, unit-
of-use, calendar packs) measurably increase medication adherence, with blister
packs the most effective format and pharmacy-delivered interventions stronger.
Underpins the clinical rationale for Dis-Chem PackMyMeds-style services. Direct
fetch returned HTTP 403 on 2026-06-13; claims drawn from PubMed/PMC search
extracts.

## Key claims

- Meta-analysis across **52 reports / ~22,858 subjects** found packaging
  interventions improve adherence with an **overall standardised mean difference
  effect size of ~0.593** (favouring treatment) — consistent with **71%
  adherence in treatment vs 63% in control** groups.
- **Blister packs were the most effective** packaging format (effect size
  ~0.802) versus pill boxes (~0.384).
- Interventions were **most effective when delivered in pharmacies**, and **less
  effective among older subjects and those with cognitive impairment** (a caveat
  for the very group that often needs DAAs).
- Linked clinical benefits in component studies: **calendar-blister packaging
  reduced blood pressure** in hypertension and **reduced HbA1c** in poorly
  controlled diabetes.
- Conclusion: evidence supports **packaging interventions as an effective
  strategy to increase adherence**.
