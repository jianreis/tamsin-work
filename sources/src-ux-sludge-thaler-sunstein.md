# Summary

Thaler's editorial defining **sludge**: friction (excessive or unjustified
effort/steps) that obstructs people from doing what is in their own interest, or
that nudges them toward what benefits the firm. The conceptual basis for
auditing a service for needless effort and for ethical (non-manipulative)
behavioural design. Cornerstone for the "remove sludge / no dark patterns"
principle in trust-and-safety UX.

# Key claims
- **Sludge** = friction that makes a beneficial action harder (or an exploitative
  action easier). Defined by "friction and bad intentions" / friction that does
  not serve the user.
- Classic sludge: hard-to-cancel subscriptions, rebates with onerous claim
  steps, confusing forms, buried opt-outs, repeated re-entry of known data.
- Good design **reduces** sludge on actions that help the user (e.g. enrolling in
  a beneficial programme, refilling chronic meds) and **does not** weaponise
  friction or dark patterns to extract value.
- Defaults are powerful; pairing a good default with low opt-out friction is
  ethical, whereas adding friction to opt-out is sludge.
- Distinct from a **nudge**, which preserves freedom of choice and steers toward
  the chooser's own benefit; sludge is the abuse of the same mechanisms.

# Verbatim excerpts
> "Whenever I am asked to sign up for something… I encounter what I have come to
> call sludge: friction that makes it harder for people to obtain something that
> will make them better off."

> Sludge has "two defining characteristics: friction and bad intentions."
