# Summary

Two internal Bigly Labs board exports (jsPDF, 2026-02-22) that together form the
**indicative visual style and treatment reference** for the future-state
scripting deliverables. **This is a design artefact — a reference for look, feel,
layout and narrative voice — not evidence.** Do not cite it for facts, statistics
or regulatory claims; cite it only for design-treatment decisions. Reliability
**C** (internal, unsourced, illustrative). Captured in full as a reusable spec at
[[design-kit/templates/future-state-blueprint-board-style]].

The two files are two **different views/sections**, not two views of one board:

1. **`...Future_State_Blueprint_Board.pdf`** — a single very wide (≈9866×2052 pt)
   landscape **journey-map board**: persona "Sarah" (39, divorced, on long-term
   medication; prefers flexible low-friction solutions) on a left rail, then four
   horizontal **moment → activity** columns for an acute pharmacist-advice flow.
2. **`...Future_State_Blueprint_Board_1.pdf`** — a single tall (≈2116×9571 pt)
   portrait **principles deck**: a "Future State Scripting" cover, a
   **Speed / Convenience / Trust** pyramid, and two stacked principle sets —
   **Customer** (purple) and **Dispenser** (grey).

# Key claims

> Treated as design-intent statements, not verified facts.

- **Brand frame.** Dis-Chem green (≈`#1a9b4a`) header band with the Dis-Chem
  wordmark + lime "+" and a "Script Re-imagined" / "Future State Scripting"
  kicker; co-branded "bigly labs" mark. Footer/cover on dark near-black.
- **Persona rail anatomy (board 1):** name (oversized), bio block, **Current
  Friction** list with lightning-bolt (⚡) bullets, **Patient Needs** statement,
  and a boxed **"How might we…"** question.
- **Sarah's current frictions (verbatim labels):** Long queues · Queue confusion ·
  Surprise costs · Low customer autonomy · Opaque order status.
- **Sarah's patient need:** "Flexible, low-friction access to repeat medication."
- **Moment row (board 1, in order):** *Seeking advice → I receive clinical
  confirmation → I collect my medication → I follow the treatment*, each with a
  clock icon and an italic moment title on a light-green band; an **Activity** row
  beneath holds bold-titled narrative cards written in first-person ("I need sinus
  relief", "Arrive at store", "Consult with the pharmacist", "I'm advised to
  consider seeing a nurse", "I'm seamlessly redirected within the ecosystem",
  "Nurse evaluates", "My care concludes efficiently", "Immediate post-collection
  guidance", "Follow-up").
- **Design principle (board 2 headline):** "We optimise for speed and convenience,
  but never at the expense of **trust**." Pyramid: **Trust** (foundation) →
  **Convenience** (built on trust) → **Speed** (optimisation), read for both
  **Customer** and **Dispenser**.
- **Customer principles (board 2):** Repeats should just, repeat · Give me all the
  variables upfront · Don't leave me in the dark · Get the basics right, every
  time · Care extends beyond the counter · My pharmacist is my front door to care.
- **Dispenser principles (board 2):** The counter is for care, not admin · Never
  turn your back on the customer · Separation & specialisation create focus ·
  Fulfillment like a production line · Remove noise to protect flow · Many inputs,
  one controlled funnel · The system prioritises the work · Proximity without
  exposure. Each principle has an italic "from → to" subhead and a **"Why this
  matters (ops)"** note.

# Verbatim excerpts

> "We optimise for speed and convenience, but never at the expense of trust.
> Unlike everyday retail, errors in the pharmacy environment have real
> consequences. If trust is compromised, convenience and speed are irrelevant,
> for customers and dispensers." (board 2)

> "How might we standardise ad-hoc repeat ordering through guided automation,
> while preserving pharmacist access?" (board 1, Sarah HMW)

> "Repeats should just, repeat — Repeat medication should behave consistently
> over time." (board 2, customer principle)

> "Proximity Without Exposure — From visible, fragmented movement to coordinated
> work behind the scenes." (board 2, dispenser principle)
