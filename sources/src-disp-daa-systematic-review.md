## Summary

Defines and evaluates dose administration aids (DAAs) — compartmentalised pill
boxes, blister/sachet multi-dose packs and automated dispensers — and reviews
their effect on adherence for self-administered oral medicines. Provides the
taxonomy and the nuance that DAAs help unintentional non-adherence specifically.

## Key claims

- **Dose administration aids (DAAs)** are a simple intervention for
  **unintentional non-adherence** to oral medicines, taking the form of
  **compartmentalised pill boxes, automated dispensing devices, blister packs
  and sachet packets**.
- DAAs primarily address **forgetfulness and regimen complexity** (unintentional
  non-adherence) rather than **intentional** non-adherence (beliefs, side
  effects, cost) — those need counselling/behavioural support instead.
- Multi-dose packaging consolidates **multiple medicines per dosing time** into a
  single labelled compartment (date/time), reducing the cognitive load of
  managing several products.
- Benefits are clearest for patients on **multiple chronic medicines** and those
  with mild cognitive or dexterity limitations.
