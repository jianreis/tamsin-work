## Summary

Evidence on whether adherence technology actually improves adherence. Meta-analyses
find **mobile apps significantly improve medication adherence** for chronic disease
(effect larger than older electronic reminders like SMS/alarms/pagers, and than
some motivational/educational interventions), though GRADE quality is **low** and
many trials rely on self-reported adherence. **Electronic adherence monitoring**
(e.g. smart pillboxes that log opening + send reminders) yields significantly better
adherence than no intervention. **Drug reminder packaging** (blister/dosette packs)
showed positive effect on at least one adherence parameter in **57%** of reviewed
studies. AI-assisted adherence support is "promising" but evidence is weak.
Takeaway: reminder/monitoring tech reliably nudges adherence behaviour, but
effect sizes vary, measurement is often weak, and clinical-outcome (not just
adherence) evidence is thinner — design for sustained engagement, not novelty.

## Key claims
- Mobile apps are associated with a **significant** improvement in medication
  adherence for chronic conditions (GRADE quality **low**).
- App effect appears **larger** than older electronic reminders (SMS, alarms,
  pagers) and some psychological/educational interventions.
- **Electronic adherence monitoring** (pillbox-open logging + reminders)
  significantly improves adherence vs none.
- **Drug reminder packaging** had a positive effect on ≥1 adherence parameter in
  **57%** of studies reviewed.
- AI tools for adherence support are "promising" but current evidence is **weak**.
- Limitations: varied effect sizes, varied controls, reliance on self-reported
  adherence; clinical-outcome evidence thinner than adherence-behaviour evidence.
