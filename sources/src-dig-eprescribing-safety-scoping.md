## Summary

Synthesis of peer-reviewed and authoritative literature on e-prescribing safety.
E-prescribing improves legibility, enables drug-interaction/allergy checking, and
reduces transcription and communication errors; embedded CDS alerts can prompt
prescription changes that prevent adverse events. But the same literature
documents real failure modes: **alert fatigue** (clinicians desensitised by
excessive, low-relevance alerts so that clinically important ones are missed),
**automation bias** (over-reliance on CDS reducing vigilance — omission and
commission errors), and **new computer-generated error types** (wrong-drug from
pick-lists, dose/default errors, dropdown mis-selection) that paper did not
produce. Much of the evidence is descriptive (error rates) rather than outcome-
focused; net safety is positive but contingent on good CDS design and governance.

## Key claims
- E-prescribing improves quality/efficiency, reduces transcription &
  communication errors, enables interaction/allergy checking and cost savings.
- CDS alerts can prompt prescription changes that prevent possible adverse events.
- **Alert fatigue**: high volume of low-relevance alerts desensitises prescribers;
  clinically relevant alerts get overlooked.
- **Automation bias**: over-reliance on CDS reduces vigilance — documented
  omission and commission errors.
- E-prescribing introduces **new error types** (e.g. pick-list/dropdown
  mis-selection, defaulted doses) absent in paper workflows.
- Evidence base is largely descriptive; outcome-level effect on harm is
  under-studied — design and governance determine net benefit.

## Verbatim excerpts
> "Alert fatigue is common, where physicians become desensitized to alerts because

> "Automation bias occurs when users over-rely on clinical decision support

> potential to harm patient safety."
