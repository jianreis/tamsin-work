# Pharmacy Script Journey — In-Depth Interviews

## Summary

First-party qualitative customer research commissioned inside Bigly Labs
(Tribe 4: Enterprise Acceleration) to understand how **in-store self-collect**
Dis-Chem customers experience the **end-to-end script journey** and where
friction can be reduced for a future-state design. Method: **10 remote, 1-hour,
one-on-one in-depth interviews**, fielded **20–25 October 2025**, with customers
who had filled a prescription in-store at Dis-Chem within the prior 30 days.
The report maps the journey across **six customer-described stages** (getting
the prescription → arriving/finding the place → handover/waiting →
information/counselling → paying/collecting → leaving/taking medication) and
records what worked well, what caused friction, and recommendations per stage.

**Scope caveat (important):** the study deliberately covers **only the in-store
self-collection path** for **chronic and acute** scripts. **Delivery,
click-and-collect, and app/web flows were explicitly excluded** and flagged for
separate follow-up research. Findings are therefore strongest on the physical
in-store experience and weakest (by design) on digital/remote fulfilment.

**Reliability — B (justification).** First-party primary research with a
documented protocol, screener, consent/NDA, named research team, and a
2-person facilitator+observer model — stronger than anecdotal D-tier signals
(app-store / HelloPeter). But it is **small-n (10), purposively recruited,
qualitative**, skewed off-peak and toward in-store regulars, so it gives
**directional sentiment and lived experience, not prevalence**. Treat counts
("7/10 acute") as sample descriptors, not population estimates. Not A-tier
(not a regulator/peer-reviewed/representative-quantitative source).

## Method & participants

- **Methodology:** qualitative in-depth interviews (IDIs); 1 facilitator + 1
  observer/note-taker per session; ~1 hour each (~10 hours total). Screener +
  recruiter brief; research protocol; signed consent & NDA. (p.48–49)
- **Fieldwork dates:** 20–25 October 2025. (p.48)
- **n = 10** Dis-Chem customers who filled an in-store script in the past 30
  days; mix of chronic-repeat and acute one-off journeys. (p.6)
- **Recruitment frame:** balanced by age (25–60), gender, province; urban &
  peri-urban across Gauteng, Western Cape, KwaZulu-Natal, Mpumalanga, Northern
  Cape. (p.6)
- **Geography:** Johannesburg 5, Cape Town 2, Durban 1, Mbombela 1, Kimberley 1.
  (p.6)
- **Gender:** Female 7/10 (70%), Male 3/10 (30%). (p.7)
- **Age:** 25–34 = 2/10; 35–44 = 4/10; 45–54 = 3/10; 55+ = 1/10. (p.8)
- **Race:** Black 6/10; Coloured 2/10; Indian 1/10; White 1/10. (p.9)
- **Script type:** Acute 7/10 (70%); Chronic 3/10 (30%). (p.10)
- **Medication-use patterns (primary):** long-term regimen users (5); mixed
  users — ongoing + OTC/supplements + occasional short courses (3); acute-only
  (1); recovery support — short-term supplements post-procedure (1). Several
  also use vitamins/OTC alongside their primary pattern. (p.13)
- **Research team:** Camilla Cicatello (researcher); Tamsin Madden (service
  designer). (p.50)

## Key claims

- The **main research question** was how in-store prescription customers
  experience the end-to-end journey and where Dis-Chem can **reduce friction and
  improve consistency** for a best-in-class future experience. (p.2)
- Scope was explicitly **Doctor → arrival → wayfinding → queue → handover →
  wait → counselling → payment/collection → exit**; **delivery,
  click-and-collect and app flows were to be studied separately**. (p.3)
- **Speed/convenience, one-stop shopping, and stock reliability** are the
  primary drivers of pharmacy choice — and the same three drivers explain why
  customers choose Dis-Chem specifically. (p.15, p.18)
- **Using multiple pharmacies is normal**: customers keep a "main" pharmacy but
  switch easily for speed, stock, or convenience — pragmatism over loyalty;
  "If I can't find something at Dis-Chem, I'll just check Clicks or Pharmet."
  (p.16)
- **Human service still matters**: friendly, knowledgeable, approachable staff
  are repeatedly named as a reason to prefer a pharmacy and set the emotional
  tone of the Dis-Chem experience. (p.16, p.19)
- **Stock dependability is a defining Dis-Chem strength**: customers "never
  leave empty-handed"; when stock is out, staff "take your details and call when
  stock comes." (p.18)
- **Customers' definition of "chronic" differs from the clinical one** — many
  describe recurring/episodic conditions (sinus, gout) as "chronic" because they
  medicate routinely; journeys should be framed around **ongoing-management
  behaviour, not medical category**. (p.11–12)
- **Stage 1 (Getting the script):** nearly everyone still receives a
  **handwritten paper script**; only two have used email/app upload and both
  found it **secondary or unreliable**. Timing follows life rhythm — chronic
  users have monthly routines; acute users fill immediately if unwell. **Errand
  bundling and payday** drive timing. (p.23)
- **Stage 1 friction — coordination gaps:** when doctors email/fax scripts,
  customers **can't tell if Dis-Chem received them**, causing double trips or
  calls; there is **no consistent confirmation system** for digital script
  hand-overs. (p.24)
- **Stage 2 (Arriving/finding place):** regulars navigate on autopilot;
  **ticketing is common but not universal** (customers notice immediately when a
  store lacks it); waits ~5–10 min off-peak but **up to ~25–30 min at month-end
  / pensioner days**. (p.27–28)
- **Stage 3 (Handover/waiting):** routine, quick handover via **ID/loyalty
  lookup**; medical-aid issues often resolved **on the counter** (e.g. a GEMS
  co-pay adjusted in person). Frictions: **profile-lookup inconsistencies across
  branches** (surname variants/duplicates, "profile not found"), **redundant
  digital steps** (asked to WhatsApp/email a script *and* hand over the paper
  copy), and **multi-profile/family-collection admin**. (p.31–32)
- **Stage 4 (Information/counselling):** **no significant frictions observed**;
  printed labels are trusted and clear; "show-and-tell" (holding the box) builds
  confidence; pharmacists tailor advice to repeat customers. (p.35–36)
- **Stage 5 (Paying/collecting):** medical-aid claims processed **automatically
  and instantly** at the till; **co-pays seen as small and fair** ("R17 versus
  R300 — that's fair"); cash used for vitamins/extras when medical-aid savings
  run low. Frictions: **till queues can exceed pharmacy queues**; **idle-staff
  perception** when cashiers chat during busy periods. (p.38–39)
- **Stage 6 (Leaving/taking medication):** integrated shopping (browse
  specials/household items) reinforces one-stop behaviour; **nearly all begin
  treatment the same day**, trusting labels + verbal explanation. Minor gap:
  desire to **pay at the dispensary counter** for script-only trips (recurs from
  Stage 5). (p.42–43)
- **Loyalty is used but not understood** — nearly all swipe the card but
  programme comprehension is low; fielded **<1 week after the Better Rewards
  launch**, so awareness was understandably low. (p.19)
- **Digital engagement is low in this cohort (by recruitment design):** only 1
  uses delivery regularly, 2 tried it once, most don't use the app (or only for
  specials); "it's about trust and routine… digital isn't rejected, it just
  doesn't yet fit their habits." One participant is **sceptical of digital after
  being scammed**. (p.19)
- **Delivery variant (1 participant):** app upload → SMS confirmation → **co-pay
  paid via the driver's card machine**; described as convenient and smooth.
  (p.31, p.38)
- **Cross-journey recommendations:** (1) digital trust & communication — confirm
  script hand-overs via SMS/app; (2) consistent ticketing & signage + peak-time
  study; (3) standardise ID/loyalty profile lookup, de-duplicate family
  profiles; (4) optional **payment at the dispensary counter** for script-only
  purchases; (5) strengthen staff training for polite, efficient, proactive
  handovers. (p.45)
- **Stated next research:** study app/delivery users to surface digital pain
  points; observational fieldwork during month-end/pensioner peaks. (p.25,
  p.40, p.46)

## Verbatim excerpts

- "Efficiency is very important to me. I do not like queues at all… I don't have
  the patience for it with the kids." — Participant 2 (p.15)
- "Life is busy, and anywhere I don't have to go to two or three shops to get
  what I need — that's where I'll go." — Participant 1 (p.15)
- "I don't like to walk to a pharmacy and then they don't have the item I'm
  looking for." — Participant 8 (p.15) / echoed by Participant 1 (p.18)
- "If I can't find something at Dis-Chem, I'll just check Clicks or Pharmet." —
  Participant 4 (p.16)
- "When staff greet you nicely, you feel like they care." — Participant 2 (p.16)
- "It's very rare when they don't have what you need — they'll take your details
  and call when stock comes." — Participant 5 (p.18)
- "I refill every month; it's part of my life." — clinically-chronic participant
  (p.12)
- "I always have sinus — I take my meds every day." / "I'm monitoring my uric
  acid levels." — perceived-chronic participants (p.12)
- "They're familiar with me and my medication — they even recommend what might
  help better." — Participant 4 (p.19)
- "I use the card every time, but I haven't really checked what it does." —
  Participant 5 (p.19)
- "I'm very sceptic of such stuff... I've been scammed a couple of times." —
  Participant 6 (p.19)
- "I plan it (getting meds) around payday or if I'm already going to the shops."
  — Participant 2 (p.23)
- "My doctor and pharmacy are right there at the mall." — Participant 4 (p.23)
- "You basically just select which one you go into and they give you a number...
  I like it because I can still browse." — Participant 1 (p.27)
- "At month-end I waited about twenty-five minutes — that's quite long when
  you're not feeling well." — Participant 5 (p.27)
- "At first with chronic, I had to pay a difference, but they helped adjust it
  with GEMS." — Participant 4 (p.31)
- "Service is efficient… you don't wait super long." — Participant 3 (p.31)

  taking the right thing." — Participant 1 (p.35)

  energy and hormones." — Participant 7 (p.35)
- "I don't need them to explain, I've been on these for years, I just check the
  label and go." — Participant 8 (p.35)
- "They process it right there, I just hand my card (medical aid card) and it's
  done." — Participant 5 (p.38)
- "I usually grab one or two things, water, sweets, something on special, before
  I pay." — Participant 4 (p.38)
- "R17 [co-pay] versus R300, that's fair." — paraphrased participant view (p.39)
- "I was comfortable knowing how to take it because not only did he tell me, it
  was also written on the label, it was quite easy to follow." — Participant 8
  (p.42)
- "I would browse in the shop… check what is on special, maybe on toilet paper
  or household products. I also visit their perfume counter." — Participant 10
  (p.42)
