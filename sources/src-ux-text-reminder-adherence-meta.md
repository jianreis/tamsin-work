# Summary

Pooled evidence that **SMS/text reminders improve medication adherence** for
chronic disease — a consistent, modest-to-moderate, replicated effect. Provides
the effect sizes and design moderators (duration, personalisation, two-way) that
justify and shape reminder/nudge patterns in the scripting journey.

# Key claims
- Thakkar et al. (2016) meta-analysis (16 RCTs, ~2,742 patients): text messaging
  **roughly doubled the odds of medication adherence** (pooled OR ≈ 2.11; 95% CI
  1.52–2.93), raising adherence from ~50% to ~68% in modelled terms.
- T2DM RCT meta-analysis (BMC Endocr Disord 2023; 9 trials, 1,121 participants):
  pooled **SMD 0.36 (95% CI 0.14–0.59)** for text reminders vs usual care.
- Coronary-heart-disease meta-analysis (2019): adherence ~**2.85× greater** in
  the texting group (RR 2.85; 95% CI 1.07–7.58); pooled MD 0.61 (95% CI
  0.37–0.85).
- **Moderators / design implications:** effects tend to be larger with
  interventions **>6 months**, with **personalised** content, and the field
  trends toward **two-way / interactive** messaging over one-way blasts.
- Reminders mainly address the **automatic-motivation / capability (forgetting)**
  driver of non-adherence (per COM-B); they do not fix cost/access barriers.
- Caveat: many trials rely on **self-report or refill-rate** adherence measures,
  which can overstate effects; heterogeneity is high in some pooled analyses.
