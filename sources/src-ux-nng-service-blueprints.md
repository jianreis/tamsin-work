# Summary

NN/g's canonical explainer set on service blueprinting — the definition article
plus the companion "5 Steps to Service Blueprinting" and "4 Key Components"
video. Defines a service blueprint as a diagram visualising the relationships
between service components (people, props/evidence, processes) tied to
touchpoints in a specific customer journey. Establishes the standard layered
anatomy and the three separating "lines" that every blueprint uses. This is the
authoritative method reference the vault's blueprint template cites.

# Key claims
- A service blueprint is "a diagram that visualizes the relationships between
  different service components — people, props (physical or digital evidence),
  and processes — that are directly tied to touchpoints in a specific customer
  journey."
- Four key component clusters: (1) customer actions, (2) frontstage / onstage
  (visible employee/tech actions), (3) backstage (invisible employee/tech
  actions), (4) support processes (internal steps and partners supporting
  employees).
- A fifth element, **physical/digital evidence**, sits across the top: the
  props and places anyone in the blueprint touches; can be involved in both
  frontstage and backstage.
- Three primary **lines**: (a) **line of interaction** — direct customer↔org
  interactions; (b) **line of visibility** — separates what the customer sees
  (frontstage, above) from what they don't (backstage, below); (c) **line of
  internal interaction** — separates contact employees from those who don't
  directly support the customer (the support processes below it).
- A blueprint is always built for **one scenario** (one customer journey), and
  is most valuable for omnichannel, complex, or cross-departmental services
  where hand-offs and silos hide failure points.
- Build in 5 steps: (1) find the right process/scenario to blueprint;
  (2) identify the customer actions that anchor the columns; (3) add frontstage,
  backstage and support actions; (4) add the lines and evidence; (5) refine and
  add "additional layers" as needed.
- Optional **additional lanes** (added per goal): time/duration, emotions,
  metrics/KPIs, regulations/policy, and pain points/opportunities.
- Blueprints can be **high-level** (strategic, few details) or **detailed**
  (operational); choose the zoom level to match the decision being made.

# Verbatim excerpts
> "A service blueprint is a diagram that visualizes the relationships between
> different service components — people, props (physical or digital evidence),
> and processes — that are directly tied to touchpoints in a specific customer
> journey."

> "The line of visibility separates all service activities that are visible to
> the customer from those that are not visible. Everything frontstage (visible)
> appears above this line, while everything backstage (not visible) appears
> below this line."

> "The line of internal interaction separates contact employees from those who
> do not directly support interactions with customers/users."

> "The last layer of a service blueprint is evidence, which is made of the props
> and places that anyone in the blueprint has an exchange with."
