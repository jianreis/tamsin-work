## Summary

Landmark NEJM study establishing that barcode verification reduces medication
errors, plus the ASHP statement recommending barcode verification across
inventory, preparation and dispensing. Evidence base for NAPPI-barcode scanning
as a dispensing control. Direct fetch returned HTTP 403 on 2026-06-13; claims
drawn from search extracts and the study's well-documented results.

## Key claims

- Barcode verification at the point of administration **electronically verifies
  the "five rights"** (right patient, drug, dose, route, time) and the Poon et
  al. NEJM study found **substantial reductions in administration errors and
  potential adverse drug events** after barcode-eMAR implementation.
- ASHP recommends **barcode verification across the medication-use process** —
  from receiving/stocking inventory, through preparation, to dispensing — as a
  core safety control.
- An **increase in self-reported near-miss errors should be expected** when
  barcode systems are first deployed (the system surfaces catches that were
  previously invisible) — a feature, not a regression.
- No system achieves **100% scan rates**; **workarounds** (smudged labels,
  un-updated barcodes, overridden warnings) limit effectiveness and must be
  designed against.
- In one UK evaluation, **patient-identification accuracy rose from 74% to
  100%** and **95% of scannable doses were verified**.
