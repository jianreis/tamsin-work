## Summary

The foundational global standard defining Good Pharmacy Practice (GPP). GPP is
"the practice of pharmacy that responds to the needs of the people who use the
pharmacists' services to provide optimal, evidence-based care." It frames
pharmacy around four major roles and sets quality standards for each, including
the dispensing of prescribed medicines. WHO/cdn.who.int domains returned HTTP
403 to automated fetch on 2026-06-13; claims below are drawn from WHO/FIP search
extracts and corroborating GPP reference papers retrieved the same day, and from
the document's well-documented public structure. Anchor source for the
end-to-end dispensing workflow and pharmaceutical-care framing.

## Key claims

- GPP defines **four major roles** of the pharmacist: (1) prepare, obtain,
  store, secure, distribute, administer, **dispense** and dispose of medical
  products; (2) provide **effective medication therapy management**; (3)
  maintain and improve **professional performance**; (4) contribute to
  **improving the effectiveness of the health-care system and public health**.
- Pharmacists should **assess and evaluate all paper or electronic
  prescriptions** received, considering the **therapeutic, social, economic and
  legal aspects** of the prescribed indications **before supplying** medical
  products to the patient.
- Pharmacists should ensure that **appropriate facilities, trained personnel,
  standard dispensing practices and documentation procedures** are in place for
  the supply and dispensing of prescribed medicines and other health-care
  products.
- Pharmacists should ensure **regular monitoring of the medicines inventory**,
  including periodic inspection for **expiration dates** and removal of outdated
  stock.
- GPP requires national professional standards (set by the national pharmacy
  organisation, regulator and government) and a focus on patient welfare.
