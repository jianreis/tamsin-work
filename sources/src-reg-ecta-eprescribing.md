# Electronic prescribing, ECTA & advanced electronic signatures

## Summary
Compliance analysis tying Regulation 33 (Medicines Act) to the Electronic
Communications and Transactions Act 25 of 2002 (ECTA). Establishes that a true
e-prescription is legally valid in SA when generated by an "electronic agent" and
signed with an Advanced Electronic Signature (AES). Vendor source but accurately
restates the statutory position; corroborated by Pharmaciae (SAPC).

## Key claims
- ECTA s13 + the definition of AES: an AES is one produced by a process accredited
  by the SA Accreditation Authority (SAAA); an AES carries a presumption of
  validity (stronger than a wet-ink signature evidentially).
- Reg 33 recognises an electronic prescription as valid where it is prepared by an
  electronic agent (as defined in ECTA) and signed with an AES per ECTA s13.
- A computer-generated prescription so signed has the same legal force as a valid
  paper prescription.
- Distinction matters: a scanned/photographed/emailed PDF of a wet-ink script is an
  electronic TRANSMISSION, not an e-prescription; for these (and fax/verbal) a
  permanent copy must be retained and authenticity verified by the pharmacist.

## Verbatim excerpts
- "Regulation 33 of the Medicines and Related Substances Act details the
  requirements of a valid prescription, and electronic prescriptions need to be
  prepared with an electronic agent as defined by the ECTA and signed with an
  advanced electronic signature as per section 13 of the ECTA."
- "An advanced electronic signature is regarded as being a valid electronic
  signature when applied properly, unless the contrary is proved, and a
  computer-generated prescription could therefore have the same legal force as any
  valid prescription."
