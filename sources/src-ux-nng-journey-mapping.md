# Summary

NN/g's canonical journey-mapping reference set ("Journey Mapping 101", "When and
How to Create Them", and the UX-mapping study guide). Defines the journey map,
its zones/anatomy, the "lens" (persona + scenario) that scopes it, and
distinguishes journey maps from experience maps and service blueprints. This is
the authoritative method reference the vault's journey-map template cites.

# Key claims
- A journey map is "a visualization of the process that a person goes through in
  order to accomplish a goal." Its purpose is shared understanding and finding
  opportunities to improve the experience.
- Every journey map needs a **lens**: (a) a **point of view / persona** (the
  actor) and (b) a **scenario** (the specific experience/goal being mapped).
  One map = one persona × one scenario.
- **Zone A (the lens):** persona + scenario/expectations. **Zone B (the heart /
  experience):** the chronological story — **phases (stages)**, **actions**,
  **thoughts**, and **emotions** (often drawn as an emotional curve). **Zone C
  (the output / insights):** opportunities, internal ownership, and next steps.
- **Phases** are high-level stages that organise the map (e.g. for e-commerce:
  discover → try → buy → use → seek support). Actions/thoughts/emotions are
  placed within each phase.
- Touchpoints (moments of contact) and channels (where/how contact happens) are
  layered in; pain points and opportunities are derived from the emotional lows.
- Journey maps should be grounded in **qualitative research** (interviews, diary
  studies, contextual inquiry), not assumptions; assumption-based maps are
  "hypothesis maps" to be validated.
- **As-is vs to-be / future-state:** an as-is map documents the current
  experience; a future-state (to-be) map envisions an ideal/improved experience
  and is used to align teams on a target.
- **Journey map vs service blueprint:** a journey map focuses on the *customer's*
  experience (what they do/think/feel); a service blueprint extends *below the
  line of visibility* to the org's frontstage/backstage actions and support
  processes that deliver that experience. Blueprints are typically the next step
  after a journey map.
- **Experience map** = a more general, product-agnostic visualisation of a human
  behaviour (no specific business/persona); a journey map narrows that to one
  persona+scenario for one organisation.
