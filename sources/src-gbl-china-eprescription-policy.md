# Summary

The policy machinery that lets China's e-prescriptions exist and flow: licensing
internet hospitals, validating e-signed + pharmacist-reviewed online scripts,
pushing prescriptions out of hospitals ("medicine separated from prescription"),
and wiring medical-insurance settlement into online platforms. Also the
guardrails: no first-visit-online, and a ban on AI auto-generating prescriptions.
Government + reputable press + academic → B.

# Key claims
- **2018 rules (14 Sept):** internet hospitals must be based on a licensed physical
  hospital; online prescriptions valid only with **real-name doctor auth +
  electronic signature + pharmacist review**; **no diagnosis/prescribing on a
  first visit** — online care limited to **follow-up for common/chronic conditions**.
- **"Medicine separated from prescription" (医药分开):** zero-markup drug policy
  (public hospitals abolished drug mark-ups by Sept 2017) created **prescription
  outflow** to retail/online pharmacies — the structural tailwind for AliHealth/
  JD Health/Ping An.
- **Insurance integration:** "Internet Plus" reimbursement parity (online = offline);
  by **July 2023, 26 provinces** ran unified medical-insurance **e-prescription
  circulation centres** linking ~**1.02m institutions and 66,300 pharmacies**;
  pilots (Chengdu) complete consult→prescription→dispense→insurance settlement in
  ~5 minutes.
- **2022 tightening:** NHC supervision rules **banned AI from auto-generating
  prescriptions** and enforced **"prescription first, drug second"** (no supplying
  a drug before its prescription is issued and reviewed).

# Verbatim excerpts
> "prohibit the use of artificial intelligence (AI) to generate prescriptions
> automatically" (NHC supervision rules, Feb 2022; reaffirmed Beijing draft, Aug 2023)

> "Medical institutions shall not use Internet Diagnosis on a patient's first
> visit." (Internet Diagnosis Administrative Measures, 2018)
