## Summary

How the uninsured/cash majority pays for scripts and the products bridging the
affordability gap. Two distinct things: (1) **medical schemes** (regulated under
the Act, must cover PMBs, include medical savings accounts on many options);
(2) **health insurance** (cheaper, primary-care-focused short-term-insurance
products — NOT medical schemes, no PMBs) such as GetSavvi, targeting people who
cannot afford a scheme. Cash patients otherwise pay SEP + regulated dispensing
fee out of pocket (see [[src-sch-sep-dispensing-fee]]).

## Key claims

- **~15.8% / ~16%** of the population can afford a medical scheme; **price is the
  leading barrier** to private healthcare.
- **Health insurance ≠ medical scheme**: insurance products cover day-to-day
  primary care (GP, dentistry, basic meds) for a lower premium and do **not**
  provide PMBs; aimed at those who cannot afford a scheme or want to top up
  day-to-day cover.
- **GetSavvi** Primary Care plans: from ~**R582/month** (2026 figure cited);
  unlimited GP consults, some chronic medication, emergency, HIV/STI testing;
  open to anyone regardless of age/medical history.
- **Medical savings accounts (MSAs)** are widely used in SA scheme options —
  members self-fund day-to-day/medicine spend from a personal savings pot
  (critiqued in literature as individualising benefits / undermining risk pooling).
- Uninsured cash patients pay **out of pocket** at SEP + regulated dispensing fee.
