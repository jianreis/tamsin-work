# Summary

AHRQ's evidence-based toolkit for communicating with **all** patients as if they
may not fully understand — the "universal precautions" stance on health literacy.
Provides the teach-back method, Ask Me 3, and plain-language guidance that
translate directly into low-literacy-friendly pharmacy UX (dosing instructions,
consent, error/safety messages).

# Key claims
- **Health-literacy universal precautions:** treat *every* patient as potentially
  at risk of not understanding; providers cannot reliably tell who understands,
  and everyone benefits from clear, actionable communication.
- **Teach-back method:** ask the patient to restate, in their own words, what
  they need to know/do; it is "a test of how well you explained, not a test of
  the patient." Improves comprehension/retention and lowers readmissions.
- **"Chunk and check":** break information into small pieces and confirm
  understanding after each, rather than at the end.
- **Ask Me 3:** prompt patients to get answers to three questions — *What is my
  main problem? What do I need to do? Why is it important that I do this?* —
  giving a minimal, action-oriented information spine.
- **Plain language:** use common words, short sentences, the active voice, and
  define necessary medical terms; design materials at a low reading grade.
- Low health literacy is associated with worse adherence, more errors, and worse
  outcomes — making clear communication a **safety** issue, not just usability.

# Verbatim excerpts
> "Teach-back is a way to confirm that you have explained information in a manner
