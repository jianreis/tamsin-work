# Summary

Gollwitzer's foundational work on **implementation intentions** — "if-then" plans
that specify *when, where and how* a person will act on a goal ("If situation Y,
then I will do Z"). A large body of evidence (incl. Gollwitzer & Sheeran's 2006
meta-analysis of 94 studies, medium-to-large d ≈ 0.65) shows they substantially
raise goal attainment, including health behaviours. Underpins "planning prompts"
and the Timely element of EAST. Used for adherence/refill prompt design.

# Key claims
- An **implementation intention** links an anticipated situational **cue** to a
  goal-directed **response**: "If situation Y is encountered, then I will
  initiate behaviour Z." This differs from a mere goal intention ("I want to X").
- Forming the plan makes the mental representation of the cue **highly
  accessible** and creates a strong cue→response link, so the behaviour is
  triggered relatively **automatically** when the cue appears — bridging the
  intention–action gap.
- Gollwitzer & Sheeran's meta-analysis (2006) of 94 independent studies found a
  **medium-to-large positive effect (d ≈ 0.65)** of implementation intentions on
  goal attainment across domains, including health.
- **Planning prompts** (actively prompting people to state when/where/how they
  will act) increase follow-through and are a low-cost intervention — e.g.
  prompting a patient to decide exactly when/where they will take or collect a
  medicine.
- Most effective for goals that require **getting started, remembering, or
  overcoming distraction** — i.e. the classic causes of medication non-adherence.
