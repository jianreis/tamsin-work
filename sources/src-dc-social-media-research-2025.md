# Summary

Internal **social-media listening study** (49-slide deck, dated 2025-12-11,
titled "Pharmacy — Social media research") analysing **public** conversation
about the **Dis-Chem pharmacy** experience: collecting pre-packed medication
in-store, repeat/chronic collection, and home delivery. Research question:
*"What themes, frustrations, expectations and comparisons do customers express
publicly about picking up pre-packed medication in-store or getting medication
delivered from Dis-Chem?"*

**Method (slide 49).** 4,475 comments on posts mentioning **rewards programmes**
across **TikTok, Facebook and Instagram** were scraped via keyword search; 3,205
were discarded as irrelevant (not pharmacy-related); the remaining **1,270
comments** were coded for sentiment, theme and sub-theme.

**Reliability = C (consumer-sentiment signal, not a rate).** Public social-media
posts are **event-driven and self-selecting** — the deck itself notes pharmacy
"is rarely talked about unless something goes wrong," so the corpus is **skewed
to negative experiences** and is **not a representative satisfaction measure**.
Treat **theme ranking, the trust-journey model, and verbatim quotes** as reliable
qualitative signal; treat **percentages and volumes as directional**. Source the
sampling frame ("posts mentioning rewards programmes") caveat below. Use as a
**signal**, never as a satisfaction statistic.

**Headline.** Public sentiment toward Dis-Chem *pharmacy* skews **strongly
negative (65% negative / 29% positive / 6% neutral)** — almost the **mirror
image** of a comparably-sized Dis-Chem *price-and-promotion* dataset (65%
positive / 24% negative / 11% neutral). Pharmacy is judged through a **healthcare
lens, not a retail one**: failures of pre-packed collection, repeats and delivery
are experienced as **disruptions to care**, not retail inconveniences, and
provoke disproportionate emotional backlash and trust loss — sometimes
**defection to Clicks or local independent pharmacies** at higher price.

## Key claims

### About the study & method (slides 2, 49)
- Research question covers in-store **pre-packed medication collection** and
  **delivery** from Dis-Chem; themes, frustrations, expectations, comparisons. (p2)
- Corpus: **4,475 scraped comments → 1,270 pharmacy-relevant**, coded for
  sentiment/theme/sub-theme; channels **TikTok, Facebook, Instagram**; sampling
  frame was **posts mentioning rewards programmes**. (p49)

### Overall sentiment (slides 4–5, 40)
- Overall pharmacy sentiment: **65% negative, 29% positive, 6% neutral**. (p4, p40)
- Comparison dataset (Dis-Chem **price & promotion**, similar size): **65%
  positive, 24% negative, 11% neutral** — nearly opposite distribution. (p5)
- Negative sentiment is driven by **service reliability, delays, communication
  breakdowns, and medication issues**; positive sentiment appears "when systems
  work as promised" (short waits, helpful staff, fast online ordering/delivery). (p4)
- Conversation is **event-driven**: "pharmacy is rarely talked about unless
  something goes wrong" → corpus skews negative. (p4)

### Five key findings (slide 6)
1. **Pack My Meds, Collections and Deliveries are treated as essential care
   systems, not conveniences** — relied on to secure medication safely/consistently;
   failures felt as care disruptions with **far lower error tolerance** than retail
   delivery. (p6)
2. **Patients judge Dis-Chem in a healthcare context, not retail** — benchmark it
   against pharmacies/medical services, not retailers. (p6)
3. **Broken expectations trigger strong emotional backlash** — because medication
   carries health risk, errors read as serious failures → loss of trust + public
   feedback. (p6)
4. **Communication failures amplify operational problems** — poor/slow comms is
   experienced as part of the same failure, escalating frustration and a sense of
   disregard. (p6)
5. **Automation increases both convenience and risk** — speeds repeats and
   notifications, but limits patient agency; rigid processes, message overload and
   **no opt-out** create frustration and a sense of lost control. (p6)

### The Patient Trust Journey model (slides 7–36)
A 5-stage emotional arc the deck uses to organise findings:
- **Calm** — customers begin with neutral/positive expectations; **assume systems
  will work because medication access is foundational**. (p8)
- **Trigger** — first cracks: waiting time only noticed when it **fails
  expectations** (long queues read as staffing/resource failure, not situational);
  being asked to **repeat information / navigate multiple steps** escalates
  frustration; collections/pre-packs/delivery **failing to save time** read as a
  **healthcare** failure; missed delivery windows shift mild frustration to
  **anxiety**; speed/efficiency praised only when it **exceeds** expectations,
  often via **competitor comparison**. (p11–15)
- **Acceleration** — high-risk failures: **medication errors experienced as safety
  threats**, not service failures; **generic substitution** read as "incorrect
  medication"; **cold-chain** concerns amplify fear (even when not breached);
  **lost/uncaptured/delayed scripts disrupt continuity of care** for chronic
  patients; **out-of-stock chronic medication** escalates to panic/anger when not
  proactively communicated. Counterweights: **visible double-checking by senior
  pharmacists** and **proactive pharmacist assistance** build reassurance. (p18–24)
- **Agitation** — when customers **cannot locate or recover orders**, perception
  shifts from inconvenience to **abandonment**; **digital breakdowns** (app/online
  failures) become blockers when no alternative path exists; **customer-service
  inaccessibility** (unanswered calls/dispensary lines) read as organisational
  inability to care; **communication overload** (calls/SMS/WhatsApp, esp. after
  opt-out attempts) becomes **perceived harassment** and loss of control —
  paradoxically the *same* digital tools can reduce effort/stress when they work. (p27–32)
- **Defection or guarded trust** — after a trust-breaking failure many **do not
  attempt recovery**; they **move prescriptions to Clicks or local independents**,
  **willing to pay significantly more** and sacrifice convenience for
  **personalised, relationship-based care**. Dis-Chem **performs best when it
  behaves like a local pharmacy** (familiarity, continuity), comparing favourably
  vs Clicks in those moments. (p35–36)

### Data appendix — theme/sub-theme distribution & sentiment
- **Main theme volume share** (p38): **In person 76%, Online 16%, Communication
  3%, Small pharmacies 3%, Recommendation 2%.**
- **Sentiment by main theme** (p40): In-person ~60% neg; **Online ~78% neg**;
  **Communication ~87% neg**; **Recommendation ~7% neg / ~85% neutral** (people
  asking where to go); **Small pharmacies ~96% neg toward Dis-Chem** (i.e. praise
  of small pharmacies = criticism of Dis-Chem). (p40)
- **Volume by main theme** (p41): In-person dominant (~510 comments), Online
  (~190), Communication (~40), Recommendation (~20), Small pharmacies (~15).
- **In-person sub-theme sentiment** (p43): most negative = **Collections (~93%)**,
  **Stock issues (~91%)**, **Wait time (~89%)**, **Pack My Meds (~73%)**, **Issue
  with medication (~77%)**; **Service** is the *least* negative in-person
  sub-theme (~40% neg / ~58% pos); **Queries** ~100% neutral. (p43)
- **In-person sub-theme volume** (p44): **Service ~220** (dominant), Wait time
  ~70, Issue with medication ~63, Transactions ~52, Collections ~30, Stock issues
  ~25, Pack My Meds ~20, Queries ~3. (p44)
- **Online sub-theme sentiment** (p46): **Issue with medication, Collections,
  Stock issues ~100% neg**; **Transactions ~90% neg**; **Pack My Meds ~96% neg**;
  **Delivery ~70% neg / ~30% pos**; **Wait time ~80% neg**; **Service ~73% neg**;
  **Queries ~88% neutral**. (p46)
- **Online sub-theme volume** (p47): **Delivery ~75** (dominant online pain), Wait
  time ~44, Service ~19, Pack My Meds ~16, Issue with medication ~13, Transactions
  ~8, Queries ~7, Collections ~4, Stock issues ~2. (p47)
