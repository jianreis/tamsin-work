# Medicines and Related Substances Act 101 of 1965 (as amended)

## Summary
The principal statute governing the registration, scheduling, sale, prescribing,
dispensing and pricing of medicines and scheduled substances in South Africa.
Administered by the Minister of Health and SAHPRA. Key amendment vehicle was the
Medicines and Related Substances Control Amendment Act 90 of 1997, which
inserted §22A (scheduling/sale control), §22C (licences to dispense/compound,
incl. dispensing doctors), §22F (generic substitution) and §22G (transparent
pricing / single exit price). Establishes the schedule system S0–S8.

## Key claims
- §14: SAHPRA must register a medicine before it may be sold/marketed; unregistered
  medicines may only be sold under defined exceptions (e.g. §21 authorisation).
- §22A: controls the sale of scheduled substances S1–S6 and sets who may
  prescribe/sell/dispense each schedule, plus oral-prescription and emergency-supply rules.
- S2–S6 may not be sold by anyone other than a medical practitioner, dentist,
  pharmacist, or a practitioner/nurse/person registered under the Health
  Professions Act 1974 acting within scope.
- In an emergency a pharmacist may sell an S2/S3/S4 substance on a non-recurring
  basis, and an S5/S6 substance in a quantity not greater than required for
  continuous use for 48 hours.
- S6 medicines may not be repeated without a new prescription and may only be sold
  if the course of treatment does not exceed 30 consecutive days.
- No person may acquire/use/possess/manufacture/supply any S7 or S8 substance (or
  manufacture specified S5/S6 substances) without a permit from the Director-General.
- §22C(1)(a): the Director-General may issue a licence to a medical practitioner,
  dentist, nurse or other registered person to "dispense or compound and dispense"
  medicines (the "dispensing doctor" licence); applicants must first complete a
  SAPC-accredited dispensing course.
- §22C(1)(b): licence to manufacture/import/export/act as wholesaler/distribute
  medicines or scheduled substances, on conditions set by the Authority (GMP/GWDP).
- §22F: a pharmacist must inform the public of generic (interchangeable
  multi-source) substitution and must substitute unless (a) the patient expressly
  refuses, (b) the prescriber has written that substitution may not be done, or
  (c) the medicine is one for which substitution is not permitted (e.g. narrow
  therapeutic index list).
- §22G: the Minister must establish a transparent pricing system with a Single
  Exit Price (SEP) — "the only price at which manufacturers shall sell medicines
  and scheduled substances to any person other than the State." No discounts,
  rebates or bonuses to non-State buyers; logistics fee + regulated dispensing fee
  are the only permitted add-ons.

## Verbatim excerpts
- §22G (per legal commentary): a SEP "shall be the only price at which
  manufacturers shall sell medicines and Scheduled substances to any person other
  than the State"; "a manufacturer, importer, distributor or wholesaler may not
  charge any fee or amount other than the single exit price."
- "In an emergency a pharmacist may sell any Schedule 5 or Schedule 6 substance in
  a quantity not greater than that required for continuous use for a period of 48 hours."
- "Schedule 6 medicines shall not be repeated without a new prescription being
  issued and may only be sold if the course of treatment does not exceed 30
  consecutive days." (S6 30-day rule)
