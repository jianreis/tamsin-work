# Summary

Reference for the Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) lens: people "hire" products/services to
make progress on a job in a given circumstance. Captures both the Christensen
"switch interview"/narrative school (the milkshake example) and the Ulwick
Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI) school (jobs decomposed into measurable desired
outcomes). Used to frame the chronic-medication job ("get my meds without
disrupting my life") in the vault.

# Key claims
- JTBD core question: "What job is the customer hiring this product/service to
  do?" A job is the progress a person is trying to make in a given circumstance,
  with functional, emotional, and social dimensions.
- Christensen's **milkshake** study: ~40% of milkshakes were bought by morning
  commuters who "hired" the shake to make a long, boring commute more bearable
  and stave off hunger — the job, not the demographic, explained the purchase.
- A well-formed job statement is **solution-agnostic and stable over time**
  (the underlying job changes slowly even as solutions change).
- **Ulwick / ODI** treats a job as a hierarchy of **desired outcomes**, each
  rated by customers on **importance** and **current satisfaction**;
  high-importance/low-satisfaction outcomes are the biggest opportunities.
  Ulwick's outcome-driven framing predates and runs parallel to Christensen's.
- Practical use: anchor design on the job and its outcomes, then evaluate
  whether a touchpoint helps the customer make progress, rather than starting
  from features.

# Verbatim excerpts
> "Customers don't buy products; they hire them to make progress in particular
> circumstances." (Christensen, *Competing Against Luck* / HBR)

> Christensen: it turned out commuters "had hired the milkshake to do" the job
> of making a long, boring commute more interesting and filling.
