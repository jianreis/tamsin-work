# India — the emerging-market template closest to SA

India is the most relevant benchmark for SA after Australia. It solved the same
problems SA faces: a **fragmented independent-chemist landscape**, **low formal
insurance penetration** (mostly cash / out-of-pocket buyers), strong chronic-refill
demand, and an **unresolved regulatory grey-zone** around online Rx sales. The
model that won was **omnichannel / conglomerate-backed**
(Apollo, Tata) rather than **venture-subsidised pure-play** (PharmEasy, which collapsed).
For a price-sensitive cash market like SA's, that is the lesson that carries.

## The core loop (and how prescription verification works)

All four players run the same loop: **teleconsult → digital prescription → order
→ deliver**, fronted by a **prescription-photo upload** for paper scripts. PharmEasy's
mechanism is representative [[src-gbl-india-epharmacy]]: the user uploads a **photo of
the paper script**, **licensed pharmacists review and verify** it (checking doctor
name, doctor registration number, patient name, date), a **phone call confirms** the
medicines before dispatch, and the delivery agent gets the script **validated again
at the dispensing store**. Verification happens **both digitally and physically**,
a pragmatic emerging-market answer to "is this script valid?" without a national
e-script rail. The **Telemedicine Practice Guidelines 2020** are the legal hinge:
they let a registered doctor issue a digitally signed e-prescription sufficient for
dispensing (excluding high-risk Schedule classes), enabling the consult→order pipe
[[src-gbl-india-epharmacy]].

## Apollo 24|7 — omnichannel (the winning model)

Apollo doesn't fight the offline channel, it **owns** it. Teleconsult with Apollo
doctors yields a digital prescription, and medicines are **fulfilled from the Apollo
Pharmacy physical store network**, the largest chain in Asia (**7,000 stores by
Nov 2025**), used as **last-mile dark stores**, enabling ~**19-minute delivery** in
metros [[src-gbl-india-epharmacy]]. The listed-company numbers (the most reliable in
the India set) show this works: **Apollo 24|7 ~75,000 orders/day in Q3 FY25** (up
from ~61,000), GMV ~₹760 Cr/quarter, and **Apollo HealthCo swung to profit**
[[src-gbl-apollo-247-results]]. The transferable insight is that a dense physical
pharmacy estate works as an asset rather than a legacy cost: turn stores into fulfilment
nodes. This maps directly onto Dis-Chem's and Clicks' store footprints
[[wiki/dischem/_index]], [[src-clicks-direct-medicines]].

## Tata 1mg — conglomerate balance sheet beats cash-burn

Tata 1mg (controlled by Tata Digital since 2021) layers a trusted **medicine-info
and drug-interaction database** and **home lab tests** onto the e-pharmacy. It
**overtook PharmEasy to ~31% market share by Sept 2023** (PharmEasy fell to ~15%)
[[src-gbl-india-epharmacy]], largely because Tata's balance sheet funded disciplined
growth while PharmEasy retrenched. FY25 revenue ~₹2,392 Cr (+22%), losses narrowing.

## PharmEasy — the cautionary tale

PharmEasy peaked at a **~$5.6bn valuation (2021)**, made debt-funded acquisitions,
**withdrew its IPO (2022–23)**, and was recapitalised in a rights round at a
valuation **slashed ~90%** [[src-gbl-india-epharmacy]]. Lesson for SA: **pure-play,
venture-subsidised e-pharmacy economics do not hold** without either deep-pocketed
backing or an existing physical/funding base. Convenience burned on a VC subsidy does
not make a durable model.

## Netmeds — platform play inside a conglomerate

Reliance acquired Netmeds (2020) and folded it into Reliance Retail / JioMart, with
**subscription/scheduled chronic refills** and fulfilment via Reliance's physical
network [[src-gbl-india-epharmacy]]. Its standalone scale is opaque (spread across
multiple entities), but the strategic pattern (e-pharmacy as a feature inside a
larger retail/loyalty offering) mirrors what Dis-Chem could do with its app +
Benefit/loyalty programme [[wiki/dischem/_index]].

## The regulatory grey-zone (very SA-relevant)

India's e-pharmacies scaled to tens of millions of users **despite ~8 years in a
legal grey-zone** [[src-gbl-india-epharmacy]]: there is **no dedicated notified
e-pharmacy law**; online sale runs under the old Drugs & Cosmetics Act (valid Rx
required for Schedule H/H1/X; pharmacist dispensing). **Draft e-pharmacy rules have
been pending since 2018**; the **DCGI issued show-cause notices to ~20 platforms in
Feb 2023**; a **powerful offline-chemist lobby (AIOCD, ~1.25m members)** resists; and
courts have repeatedly pressed the government to finalise policy. Platforms lean on
the **"we're just an intermediary for licensed pharmacies"** defence. This dynamic of
**fast-moving operators, an inert regulator, court pressure, and an incumbent-pharmacy
lobby** is a near-exact template for SA's SAHPRA/Pharmacy-Council environment
[[wiki/sa-regulatory/_index]].

## What to borrow for SA

- **Omnichannel: turn the store estate into fulfilment/dark-store nodes** for fast
  delivery (Apollo); Dis-Chem/Clicks already have the footprint.
- **Dual prescription verification** (digital pharmacist review **+** physical
  validation at dispense) as a pragmatic answer where there's no national e-script.
- **Teleconsult → e-prescription → order → deliver** as one integrated flow, with
  clear exclusions for high-risk scheduled drugs.
- **E-pharmacy as a feature inside a loyalty/retail offering** (Netmeds/Reliance),
  which fits Dis-Chem's loyalty + app strategy.
- **Conglomerate/anchored economics over VC subsidy**, where durability comes from an
  existing base rather than burned convenience.

## What doesn't transfer

- **PharmEasy-style growth-at-all-costs** funded by venture subsidy, the explicit
  anti-pattern.
- **India's specific regulatory limbo** is a *risk to navigate* rather than a model. SA
  should aim for clearer rules, but should expect the same incumbent-lobby and
  regulator-lag dynamics [[wiki/sa-regulatory/_index]].
- **Sheer population density** that makes 19-minute metro delivery economic does not
  hold across SA's geography [[wiki/consumer-context/_index]].

## Open questions

- Indian "registered user" counts are company marketing claims (registrations ≠
  active buyers); only Apollo's listed disclosures are firm.
- Whether India ever finalises e-pharmacy rules — and in which direction (enable vs
  restrict) — is unresolved as of 2026.
