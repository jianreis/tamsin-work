# Patient counselling & health literacy

The final dispensing step, handover, is where safety either transfers to the
patient or is lost. A correctly dispensed medicine that the patient cannot use
correctly is still a failed outcome. GPP makes counselling a defined dispensing
function: the patient must leave knowing **what the medicine is, how and when to
take it, and what to expect** [[src-disp-msh-dispensing-cycle]], and pharmacists
should provide information considering the patient's full circumstances
[[src-disp-fipwho-gpp]]. In SA's GPP this is **Phase 3: provision of information
and instructions to the patient** (see [[dispensing-process-steps]]).

## Teach-back: confirming understanding, not assuming it

The single highest-leverage counselling technique is **teach-back**: asking the
patient to **explain the instructions back in their own words**
[[src-disp-teachback-ahrq]]. It is an **evidence-based health-literacy
intervention** because it tests *the clarity of the explanation*, not the
intelligence of the patient. If the patient cannot restate it, **the pharmacist
re-explains differently**, since responsibility for clear communication sits with the
professional [[src-disp-teachback-ahrq]]. The difference from the reflexive "do
you understand?" is decisive: a yes/no question confirms nothing, while
teach-back surfaces the actual gap. In a study of pharmacist-delivered teach-back
at discharge, **93.7% of patients reported increased confidence** in their
medication knowledge [[src-disp-teachback-ahrq]].

## Health literacy and label comprehension

Many patients struggle to understand standard medicine instructions; low health
literacy is a strong predictor of misuse and non-adherence. Best practice:

- **Plain language at ~6th-grade reading level** for all patient-facing materials
  [[src-disp-teachback-ahrq]].
- **Open-ended questions** ("tell me, in your own words, how you'll take this")
  rather than closed ones [[src-disp-teachback-ahrq]].
- **Clear, unambiguous label directions**: avoid jargon and ambiguous
  abbreviations, which are a known source of error
  [[src-disp-msh-dispensing-cycle]].
- **Active listening, empathy and eye contact**: counselling quality is a
  relationship, not a script [[src-disp-teachback-ahrq]].

Counselling is also the place to address **intentional** non-adherence (beliefs,
fears, side effects, cost) that packaging cannot fix
[[src-disp-conn-packaging-meta]], and it forms the patient-facing edge of MTM's
medication-related action plan [[src-disp-mtm-five-elements]].

## Multilingual & SA considerations

SA's counselling challenge is acute: **11 official languages**, wide variation in
literacy and health literacy, and patients whose first language is rarely the
language on the label. Best-practice principles above (plain language,
teach-back, pictograms) apply with extra force. **Pictogram-based labelling and
counselling** (well-studied in the SA context) and **language-matched
counselling** are key enablers of comprehension for low-literacy and non-English
patients. Teach-back is **language-agnostic**: it works in any language the
pharmacist and patient share, which makes it especially valuable here.

## Design implications

- **Make teach-back a designed step, not an optional courtesy.** Whether in-store
  or via WhatsApp/video handover, the flow should prompt the patient to confirm
  *how* they'll take the medicine, and route to re-explanation if they can't.
- **Multilingual by default.** Labels, leaflets, reminders and counselling
  prompts should be available in the patient's language, with **pictograms** for
  low-literacy users, a core accessibility requirement for SA, not a nice-to-have.
- **Plain-language everything.** Hold all patient-facing copy (app, SMS, labels,
  leaflets) to ~6th-grade reading level.
- **Counselling can be partly asynchronous.** Short, structured video or chat
  counselling can extend reach to delivery/remote patients, but high-alert
  medicines and first-time chronic starts should trigger live pharmacist contact.
- **Capture the counselling event** in the record (what was counselled,
  confirmed via teach-back), both for continuity and as a safety barrier.
