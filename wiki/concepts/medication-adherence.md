# Medication adherence

**Adherence** is the extent to which a patient takes a medicine as prescribed —
the right dose, at the right time, for the full course (for chronic disease,
indefinitely). It is the **core outcome** of the whole scripting experience:
every step from submit to refill exists so that the medicine is actually taken
and the condition is controlled. A perfectly smooth dispense that ends in an
abandoned blister pack is a failure.

Adherence routinely fails — roughly half of chronic medicines are not taken as
prescribed worldwide — but the framing matters. **Non-adherence is rarely
apathy.** It is mostly the product of:

- **System friction** — running out before a repeat is sorted, re-script visits,
  travel/cost to collect, stock-outs, confusing regimens, poly-pharmacy.
- **Cost** — co-pays and cash prices that force rationing.
- **Stigma** — for HIV, mental-health, contraception and other sensitive
  conditions, the fear of being seen collecting is itself a barrier.
- **Comprehension** — not understanding how or why to take it, across 12
  languages and varied health literacy.

Reframing non-adherence as a *design problem* (remove friction, default the
repeat, package by dose, protect privacy, counsel in-language) rather than a
patient failing is the central move for this project. It is the outcome the
[[wiki/concepts/chronic-medicine-flywheel|chronic-medicine flywheel]] is built to serve.

## Design stance
Treat adherence as the north-star metric, not a feature. Attack the *causes* —
friction, cost, stigma, comprehension — by default rather than bolting on
reminders. The best adherence intervention is a refill the patient never had to
think about plus a dignified, in-language hand-over.
